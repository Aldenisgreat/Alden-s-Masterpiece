## 2. how-eye-works — How Your Eye Works (4 steps · ~60s)

**Info Pool** (visual elements for chapter agent to use):
- Visual: Camera analogy diagram (light enters → lens → back wall)
- Visual: Simplified eye cross-section
- Visual: Millions of tiny cells (photoreceptors) on retina
- Visual: Molecule changing shape (switch flipping from off to on)
- Term: "photoreceptors" = light-catching cells
- Term: "retinal" = special molecule related to Vitamin A

**Development plan**:

- step 1 (~12s) — Camera analogy: light enters eye, hits retina
- step 2 (~15s) — Zoom into retina, show millions of photoreceptor cells
- step 3 (~18s) — Inside a cell: molecule changes shape when light hits (switch analogy)
- step 4 (~15s) — Switch flip sends signal to brain, millions of switches working all day

**Narration excerpt**:
> "Your eye works kind of like a camera. Light comes in the front, goes through the lens, and hits the back wall. That back wall is called the retina."

## 3. staring-too-long — What Happens When You Stare Too Long (5 steps · ~60s)

**Info Pool** (visual elements for chapter agent to use):
- Visual: Switches flipping at relaxed pace (normal blinking)
- Visual: Switches flipping rapidly (overload)
- Visual: Supply shelf of switches emptying
- Visual: Brain with volume knob turning down
- Metaphor: "Like muting a TV that's been too loud for too long"

**Development plan**:

- step 1 (~10s) — Normal blinking: switches flip, new ones come in, everything's fine
- step 2 (~12s) — Staring at bright light: switches flip too fast, no breaks
- step 3 (~12s) — Cell runs out of switches, can't respond to light anymore
- step 4 (~14s) — Brain turns down the volume on that spot (volume knob visual)
- step 5 (~12s) — Tired cells + ignoring brain = interesting situation

**Narration excerpt**:
> "But when you stare at something bright — like a lamp or the sun — the switches start flipping way too fast. No breaks. No time to reset."

---

## 4. ghost-image — The Ghost Image (4 steps · ~60s)

**Info Pool** (visual elements for chapter agent to use):
- Visual: White wall with light hitting all cells equally
- Visual: Split screen — fresh cells vs tired cells
- Visual: Brain receiving mixed message
- Visual: Color wheel showing red → cyan flip
- Concept: Complementary colors (red ↔ cyan, green ↔ magenta, blue ↔ yellow)

**Development plan**:

- step 1 (~12s) — Look away at white wall, light hits all cells equally
- step 2 (~15s) — Split screen: fresh cells strong, tired cells weak
- step 3 (~15s) — Brain gets mixed message, creates ghost image
- step 4 (~18s) — Color flip: red-detecting cells tired, brain sees cyan

**Narration excerpt**:
> "Here's the cool part — the colors flip. Say you stared at something red. Your red-detecting cells are the ones that got tired. When you look at white light, green and blue cells work fine, but red is weak. So your brain sees what's left — cyan. The opposite of red."

---

## 5. eye-fixes-itself — Your Eye Fixes Itself (4 steps · ~30s)

**Info Pool** (visual elements for chapter agent to use):
- Visual: Support layer (RPE) behind retina
- Visual: Cartoon conveyor belt collecting used molecules
- Visual: Labeled diagram of retina cross-section with RPE
- Visual: Clock ticking (30 seconds to 2 minutes)
- Term: "RPE" = Retinal Pigment Epithelium (support layer)

**Development plan**:

- step 1 (~8s) — Introduce RPE: thin layer of cells behind retina
- step 2 (~10s) — RPE collects used molecules, resets them, sends them back
- step 3 (~7s) — Timeline: 30 seconds to couple minutes
- step 4 (~5s) — Not a bug, a feature: eyes adjust to sunlight and moonlight

**Narration excerpt**:
> "The RPE collects the used-up molecules, resets them, and sends them back to your photoreceptors. Same molecules, recycled over and over."

## 6. fun-extras — Fun Extras (3 steps · ~30s)

**Info Pool** (visual elements for chapter agent to use):
- Visual: Painting with complementary colors side by side
- Visual: Sun with caution icon
- Visual: Character at window, "try it!" text
- Fact: Painters use complementary colors to make each other look brighter
- Warning: Sun can permanently damage retinal cells

**Development plan**:

- step 1 (~10s) — Painters use complementary colors, same science
- step 2 (~10s) — Don't stare at the sun, permanent damage
- step 3 (~10s) — Try it later: stare at bright window, then white wall

**Narration excerpt**:
> "Fun fact — painters have known about this for a long time. They put complementary colors next to each other because they make each other look brighter. Same science."
